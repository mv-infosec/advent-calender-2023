Day 2: Linguistic Tomfoolery

Your friend has decided to play an April Fool's prank, and has taken your computer and decided to change your computer's language to Chinese. Unfortunately, your friend forgot to consider two factors.

  • It's not April Fool's
  • Your friend didn't change your computer language, but rather encrypted all your files with a certain "unkown" cipher.

Coincidentally, you have a big paper due in your Spanish class tomorrow, as well as an English paper due in 3 days. You start to panic, and beg your friend to decrypt your files.

The only information your friend gives you about the cipher is that each letter corresponds to a certain Chinese character. Your friend says they will unlock your computer if you decrypt 2 passages of text, each with a different cipher. The first passage (part 1) is only in English, while the second passage (part 2) has one paragraph in English and one paragraph in Spanish (both paragraphs use the same cipher, the only difference is that the English version of the cipher has no mapping for ñ)

Your friend says that all letters in both parts are undercase, and that punctiation is left as is. Your friend also says that they have excluded the letters á, é, í, ó, ü, and ú in the Spanish cipher.

You wonder if this was really an "April Fool's prank". You also wonder if your friend is really still your friend.

Below are the two parts to this problem. Each part contains one half of the flag. It won't say infoSecClub{text}, but rather text will be split into to. It's up to you to put back together "text" and put it into infoSecClub.

E.g. if the flag was split as "linguistic_" in part one and "mayhem" in part 2, you would submit your solution as infoSecClub{linguistic_mayhen}

If you are really unsure which part is considered part of the flag, you may submit the decoded message (both parts) as your solution for credit. Remember, parts 1 and 2 have different ciphers, and part 2 has the same cipher for both English and Spanish portions.
